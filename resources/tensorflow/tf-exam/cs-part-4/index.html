    Week 1: Sequences and Prediction

    Learn about the basics of time series data, and how to prepare data to feed it to the model.

    A time series data is an ordered sequence of values that are usually equally spaced of time. Time series data can be univariate or multivariate time series.

    • Univariate Time Series Data is a type of data that consists of a single observable characteristic. For example,

    • weather forecasts, etc.

    • Multivariate Time Series Data is a type of data that consists of more than one Univariate Time Series Data.

    • For example, the global temperature and the CO2 level, population growth of japan (birth and death rate), etc.

    What are time series for?

    Time series can help to forecast data and help to make its observer make better planning. Make a backward projection to see what the data would be before the time of the collection of data. We can also use time series to fill the gap of the data that aren't available within the period of time of the data. Additionally, time series can also be used for detecting abnormally.

    What does time series data look like?

    Time series data usually consists of several patterns

    • Trends: is the direction that the data is moving
    • Seasonality: are predictable moving intervals of data
    • White noise: are unpredictable randomness in the data
    • Autocorrelation: is a representation of similarity between a given time and a lagged version of itself over a time period. A lag indicates the distance of both time events.

    How to calculate the result of a trained time series model?

    • Errors: is the difference between the forecast values and the actual values errors = forecasts - actual.
    • MSE: or mean-square-error is the average of the square values of the errors np.square(errors).mean(). The reason to square the errors is to eliminate the negative values from the errors. Otherwise, the average value could be lesser than the actual value.
    • RMSE: is the root value of the MSE np.sqrt(mse)
    • MAE: or mean absolute deviation it uses the average of the absolute errors. The benefit of MAE over MSE and RMSE, is that MAE doesn't penalize large errors too much. Use np.abs(errors).mean() for the calculation.
    • MAPE: mean ratio between the absolute error and the absolute value. Indicates the size of the error compared to the actual value. Can be calculate with np.abs(errors / x_valid).mean().

    Common Forecasting Methods

    • Naive Forecast: uses the previous value as its current value
    • Moving Average: the average of values over a fixed period of time
    • Differencing: is the difference between the value at the T and the value at an earlier period of time. The value can be from a period of a year, a month, or any period earlier.
